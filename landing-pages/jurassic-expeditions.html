Jurassic Expeditions


https://jurassic-expeditions.netlify.app

Early Nov 2023 - Early Dec 2023

Collaboration with Carl Leon


Intro

In this project, my class was tasked with designing and coding a functional website meant to handle customer service functionality for Jurassic Park if it was a real attraction. I handled this project with my partner Carl Leon, who helped with site design and HTML. The two of us were also asked to collaborate with a UX team, which was creating an app. Leon and I's site was asked to focus on Expeditions, a branch of Jurassic Park which handled rides and tourist attractions.

Prototyping


Originally, I had been in charge of handling the overall aesthetic design of the site. I had wanted it to show a clean and sleek design. Leon and I had wanted the site to be intuitive and easy to navigate, but the amount of repetition ended up creating a lazy look to the final product.


Colorways and Aesthetic



My own preferences for site design clashed not only with the color palette we were later instructed to use, but also with the UX team's designs that we were asked to collaborate with later. Our UX team loved the fun colours of the palette; the colours which I'd been actively avoiding. However, I decided to compromise and keep our formatting but change the colourway. With the help of Leon, our site managed to pull itself together and match with our UX team while not being too horrible on the eyes. I also stressed psychological color association: This site was intended for users to purchase from. We veered away from reds and associated clickables with yellow to distinguish them easier.



Final Site


The final site mostly reflects what was expected of it in the original prototyping. Some of the original flow was changed last minute because of errors in the CSS, which overall made the site seem stiff. While it is passable for a first large-scale site, the design and user flow needs work.


Result Quality


Success


This site taught Leon and I a lot about how to form a site meant to actually be interactive. With our focus on how customers would receive the site, it taught us to include accessibility features (such as information on whether some experiences are disability-accessible, or remembering to avoid layering colors which fall within the same spectrum of colorblindness).

Failure


Our site isn't very interesting or entertaining. We very much failed to have an intriguing, characteristic design; it definitely feels elementary. Some of the contrast on the site is poor, and a lot of it has slow loading time. It definitely could've been improved with some variation. In addition, the Jurassic Park branding didn't show very well through our design; the site felt vaguely Jurassic Park inspired, rather than themed.


Takeaways


I definitely feel that Leon and I were able to pinpoint our strengths and weaknesses during this project. In the future, I knew I wanted to create something with a more interesting design and better utilise appropriate colors for what I was creating. Also, Carl and I both felt obligated to take up the entire screen width of the PC we were coding on, which also comprimised quality. In future projects, I have avoided this issue by centering everything in a way that still allows it to look good.



Revision

Explanation


Four or five months later, I've come back to this project with new experience and viewpoints of how an interesting and engaging site would look. I decided to prototype it with my new experience to see the contrast in my design from the beginning of my programming journey to the end. This production of this prototype is not in collaboration with Carl Leon or any members of our previously partnered UX designers. This revision is still currently a work in progress, so the prototype I currently have is still very bare bones.



Prototype

Honestly, my main goal while making this potential prototype for Jurassic Expeditions was for it to have no resemblance to the initial site. With this rendition, I wanted to see how far my imagination could extend past the first version of it I put into the world and give it a unique touch.


Changes I made and why




Home


First, I knew that I wanted the home page to have more personality. I liked the initial header style that we came up with, but I felt that as a background image, the branding of Jurassic Park didn't really come through much. As my first step, I centered the branding without any distraction. Next, I made navigation a lot easier. Instead of having to jump to different parts of the page or scroll through while reading a bunch of descriptions, I added links to the top which would immediately take you to a separate page collecting options of the topic. The rest of this page has a unique and interesting flow, and it's meant to appear more like a large advertisement than an actual showcasing of options like our first site was.



Staff


For the staff page, I felt like the initial layout felt very rigid and unplanned. Partially because it was (adding our UX Designers was a last minute thing), but anyhow. Instead, the idea was to have it flow similar to a credits scene. Horizontal formatting can often feel easier to read through in small segments like these because in English, we read from left to right. I followed that logic and placed individual worker profiles horizontally so they're easier and less jarring to skim through.
